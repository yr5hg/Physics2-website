Chapter 28: Direct Current Circuits

Direct Current (DC)

Direct current (DC) is the flow of electric charge in a constant direction. It is produced by sources like batteries. Key points include:

  • The current's direction is steady.
  • The battery serves as a source of electromotive force (emf).

Electromotive Force (emf)

The electromotive force (\( \mathcal{E} \)) is the maximum voltage a battery can provide:

$$ V = \mathcal{E} - Ir $$

Where:

  • \( \mathcal{E} \): emf of the battery.
  • \( r \): Internal resistance of the battery.
  • \( I \): Current in the circuit.

Resistors in Series and Parallel

In Series: The equivalent resistance is the sum of the resistances:

$$ R_{eq} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + \dots $$

In Parallel: The reciprocal of the equivalent resistance is the sum of the reciprocals:

$$ \frac{1}{R_{eq}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} + \dots $$

Kirchhoff’s Rules

Kirchhoff's rules are fundamental for analyzing complex circuits that cannot be simplified using series and parallel combinations alone. The two rules are:

1. Junction Rule

The Junction Rule states that the total current entering a junction must equal the total current leaving the junction. This rule is based on the principle of conservation of electric charge:

$$ \sum I_{in} = \sum I_{out} $$

Example: If three currents \( I_1 \), \( I_2 \), and \( I_3 \) meet at a junction with \( I_1 \) entering and \( I_2, I_3 \) leaving, the equation becomes:

$$ I_1 = I_2 + I_3 $$

2. Loop Rule

The Loop Rule states that the sum of all potential differences (voltages) around any closed loop in a circuit must be zero. This rule is based on the principle of conservation of energy:

$$ \sum V = 0 $$

When applying the Loop Rule:

  • If traversing a resistor in the same direction as the current, subtract \( IR \) (voltage drop).
  • If traversing a resistor against the current, add \( IR \) (voltage gain).
  • If traversing a battery from the negative to the positive terminal, add the emf (\( \mathcal{E} \)).
  • If traversing a battery from the positive to the negative terminal, subtract the emf (\( \mathcal{E} \)).

Example: In a simple loop containing a battery (\( \mathcal{E} \)) and two resistors (\( R_1, R_2 \)) in series, the equation becomes:

$$ \mathcal{E} - I R_1 - I R_2 = 0 $$

Simplify to find the current:

$$ I = \frac{\mathcal{E}}{R_1 + R_2} $$

Combined Application

In more complex circuits with multiple loops and junctions:

  • Apply the Junction Rule to find relationships between currents at junctions.
  • Apply the Loop Rule to write equations for each independent loop.
  • Solve the resulting system of equations to find the unknown currents and voltages.

RC Circuits

An RC circuit consists of a resistor and a capacitor in series. The capacitor charges or discharges exponentially over time:

Charging:

$$ q(t) = Q(1 - e^{-t/RC}) $$

Discharging:

$$ q(t) = Qe^{-t/RC} $$

Where \( \tau = RC \) is the time constant.
