Chapter 24: Gauss's Law

Electric Flux

Electric flux (\( \Phi_E \)) measures the number of electric field lines passing through a surface. It is given by:

$$ \Phi_E = EA \cos\theta $$

Where:

  • \( E \): Electric field strength.
  • \( A \): Surface area.
  • \( \theta \): Angle between the field and the surface normal.

Key points:

  • Maximum flux occurs when the surface is perpendicular to the field (\( \theta = 0^\circ \)).
  • Flux is zero when the surface is parallel to the field (\( \theta = 90^\circ \)).
  • Units of flux: \( N \cdot m^2 / C \).

Gauss's Law

Gauss's Law states that the net electric flux through a closed surface is proportional to the total charge enclosed within it:

$$ \Phi_E = \frac{q_{in}}{\epsilon_0} $$

Where:

  • \( q_{in} \): Net charge enclosed within the surface.
  • \( \epsilon_0 \): Permittivity of free space (\( 8.85 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{C}^2/\text{N} \cdot \text{m}^2 \)).

Applications:

  • If no charge is enclosed, the net flux is zero.
  • Gaussian surfaces are chosen to simplify electric field calculations.

Applications of Gauss's Law

Gauss's Law is applied to symmetric charge distributions to calculate electric fields:

  • Spherical charge distributions: Used for fields of point charges or spheres.
  • Line of charge: Field around a charged wire or rod.
  • Plane of charge: Field created by an infinite charged plane.

Conductors in Electrostatic Equilibrium

When a conductor is in electrostatic equilibrium:

  • The electric field inside the conductor is zero.
  • The charge resides on the surface of the conductor.
  • The electric field outside the surface is perpendicular and proportional to surface charge density:

    • $$ E = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0} $$

  • On irregular surfaces, the charge density is higher where the radius of curvature is smaller.
