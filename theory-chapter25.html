Chapter 25: Electric Potential

Electric Potential and Potential Difference

Electric potential describes the potential energy per unit charge (\( V = \frac{U}{q} \)). It represents the work done to move a charge within an electric field:

$$ W = q \Delta V $$

Key points:

  • The electric potential is a scalar quantity.
  • 1 volt (V) equals 1 joule per coulomb (1 J/C).
  • The electric potential is independent of the test charge value.
  • The electric field can be interpreted as the rate of change of electric potential:

    • $$ E = -\frac{dV}{dx} $$

Potential Difference in a Uniform Electric Field

In a uniform electric field, the potential difference between two points is given by:

$$ \Delta V = -E \cdot d \cdot \cos\theta $$

Key points:

  • Electric field lines always point in the direction of decreasing potential.
  • A positive charge loses potential energy when moving in the field direction.
  • A negative charge gains potential energy when moving in the field direction.

Electric Potential Due to Point Charges

The electric potential created by a point charge is:

$$ V = k_e \frac{q}{r} $$

Where:

  • \( V \): Electric potential (V).
  • \( k_e \): Coulomb's constant (\( 8.99 \times 10^9 \, \text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{C}^2 \)).
  • \( q \): Charge magnitude (C).
  • \( r \): Distance from the charge (m).

The potential due to multiple charges is the algebraic sum of the potentials from each charge:

$$ V = \sum_{i} k_e \frac{q_i}{r_i} $$

Electric Potential in Conductors

In electrostatic equilibrium:

  • The potential is constant across the surface of a conductor.
  • There is no potential difference inside a conductor (\( \Delta V = 0 \)).
  • The surface of a conductor acts as an equipotential surface.
